An Overview of "Public Charge"

The purpose of Form I-944 is to help USCIS determine whether an applicant is likely at any time to become a public charge. What does it mean that an applicant is "likely at any time to become a public charge"? TIt means USCIS thinks the applicant will likely, at any point in the future, not have enough income/assets/resources to support him/herself and will become a public charge. (A public charge is someone who receives one or more public benefits for more than 12 months, in total, within any 36-month period). Such an applicant may be denied based on the public charge inadmissibility ground. Source: Public Charge Fact Sheet, Public Charge Fact Sheet, USCIS Public Policy Manual Chapter 3, USCIS Policy Manual Public Charge Appendix Page 1 and Greenbroad suggestion.  Source: Public Charge, USCIS Policy Manual Chapter 10, USCIS Policy Manual Chapter 9 How does USCIS determine if an applicant is "likely at any time to become a public charge"? USCIS considers the following categories of factors when making the determination:

›The Definition of "Annual Gross Income"
Annual Gross Income

Annual gross income is the total income before any adjustment, taxes, or deductions. This includes wages from employment. This is line 6 â€œtotal incomeâ€ on the 1040 Internal Revenue Service (IRS) form. On a Wage and Tax Statement, Form W-2, the total income is in line 1 â€œWages, tips, other compensation.â€ However, a W-2 does not include income from bank account interest, stocks and bond interest, or other income a person may be receiving outside of employment. If a person has multiple jobs with multiple corresponding Forms W-2, the income is added to find the total gross income.

Lawful Sources of Income

USCIS only considers income sources from legal employment or transactions. USCIS does not consider income derived from illegal activities, whether illegal under federal law or state law, in the calculation of gross annual household income. These sources include, but are not limited to income gained illegally from: gambling, drug sales or trafficking, money laundering, prostitution, and alien smuggling.

Any income derived from unlawful sources must be deducted from the applicantâ€™s household annual gross income.

However, income from unauthorized employment should be included in the applicantâ€™s household annual gross income.
